About Signify

Why we built Signify

Sign language shouldn't be a privilege. We built Signify so that anyone — anywhere, for free — can begin their journey into the Deaf community's beautiful language.

Our Mission

Education without walls

Signify was born from a simple belief: communication is a human right. Sign language opens doors to an entire community — and learning it should never require money, a classroom, or special access.

We've built a platform that's as easy to use as it is beautiful, so the focus stays on learning, not navigating menus.

GIF-based visual learning that shows every hand movement clearly and accurately.
Accessible to anyone with an internet connection — no account, no paywall, no catch.
Covers alphabets, numbers, and essential everyday vocabulary to get you started fast.
The Deaf Community

A vibrant culture, not a condition

Deafness is not a disability — it's a different way of experiencing the world. The Deaf community has its own rich culture, history, humour, and art. Sign language is at the heart of it.

Learning even a few signs shows respect, builds bridges, and invites connection across what can seem like an insurmountable barrier.

Over 70 million Deaf people use sign language as their primary language worldwide.
Deaf individuals excel in every field — technology, arts, sports, academia, and beyond.
About Sign Language

A complete, complex, living language

ASL — American Sign Language — is not a simplified version of English. It's a fully independent language with its own grammar, syntax, idioms, and regional dialects, developed naturally by Deaf communities over centuries.

Sign languages are three-dimensional, using space, movement, and facial expressions simultaneously to convey meaning that would take many words to express in a spoken language.

Sign languages use 3D space around the body — not just hands.
Facial expressions carry grammatical information, not just emotion.
There are over 300 distinct sign languages used around the world.
Did You Know?

Fascinating facts about sign language

Babies Babble with Hands

Deaf babies exposed to sign language babble with their hands — the same developmental stage as hearing babies babbling with their voices.

Boosts Cognitive Skills

Learning sign language activates spatial reasoning and enhances memory — bilingual sign language users show measurable cognitive advantages.

Hollywood is Taking Notice

Films like CODA and A Quiet Place have brought sign language into mainstream culture, sparking a global wave of interest in ASL.

Officially Recognised in the 1960s

Linguist William Stokoe proved in 1960 that ASL is a complete language — a landmark moment that changed how the world sees Deaf culture.

A Career Advantage

Sign language interpreters, educators, healthcare workers, and counsellors with ASL skills are in high demand across every sector.

Universal Respect

Knowing even basic signs — Hello, Thank You, Sorry — signals respect and inclusion to Deaf individuals you encounter every day.
